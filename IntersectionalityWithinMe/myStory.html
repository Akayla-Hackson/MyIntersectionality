Introduction: High school is a time of great change. It is a period of time in which we grow not just physically but also mentally and emotionally. Most people dread the topic of high school. For many it was a time of social awkwardness, endless pimples, and humiliation. For me, it was the most transformative time in my life. I had many of the same fearful and awkward moments all high school kids do but, for me, I accepted and embraced both my racial and sexual identity. However, before I get into what I learned, I must begin with where I started.

Middle School: I went to a public middle school with a large and diverse student body. I had no real concept of race or sexuality at that time. None of the students really talked about these things. However, we understood the concept of class. The rich kids lived in big houses in certain neighborhoods and the poor kids lived in apartments in other areas. After eighth grade, I decided to transfer to a small private high school called Pinewood. Now when I say small, I mean tiny! The school had a total of 200 students, therefore, each grade only had approximately 50 students.

Race: I went from having an economically diverse 400 students in my grade to having 50 students whose parents were all in the 1%. The racial diversity also diminished with the student count. When I first set foot on campus, there were only two black kids, and I was one of them. The other ended up being one of my best friends, Chloe. Chloe was a year older than me, but we were both bi-racial, on the basketball team, and we wore the same loose fitting style of clothes. Everyone thought we were related, we were so similar. Anytime the diversity of Pinewood would be brought up by students, the students body would say the Pinewood only had 3/4ths of a black person because Chloe was half black and I was one fourth black. The student body really only saw us as white. In their eyes, to be considered black we had to have much darker skin, talk a certain way, and be from a poor neighborhood. Since we were not any of those things, we were considered white. This was fine with me because at that age, I just wanted to fit in. Yet it was during this time period that I also became more aware of racism and prejudice.

My mother is half black and she told me about a black man she worked with at Google, who lived in the city of Atherton, the city neighboring Pinewood. Atherton also being one of the most expensive cities to live in the Bay Area. This man said that when he first moved into Atherton, at least once a week, and usually at night, he would get pulled over by a police officer for no reason. The police officer would ask why he was in the area while checking his identification. Though I personally never experienced racism like this, his story really stuck with me. It showed me a stereotype. People have already made up their mind on what a rich person looks like: a white man. Everytime the man got pulled over, the police officer had a preconceived assumption that the man did not belong in the neighborhood because he was black. High school was a time of growing racial consciousness for me. However, it was the awareness and acceptance of my sexual identity that was truly transformative for me in high school.

Sexual Awareness: My parents encouraged me to go to a private high school to get a great education. Another reason I went to Pinewood was because they had an elite basketball program and I wanted to pursue basketball at the college level. I was surprised to find that the basketball team had many lesbians and bisexuals. In my freshman and sophomore years we tried to hide our sexuality from the other kids at school. We did not want the kids at school talking about our sex lives partially because we were still trying to figure it out for ourselves. Coming out is a very scary concept for many people! We do not know how the people we love will act, let alone all the other people at school. For me, admitting I was gay felt like I had dissapointed my parents. It was a horrible feeling. Years after coming out my mom explained to me that before I came out I was an extremely angry girl. I was defensive and behaved like I always had a chip on my shoulder. She explained that after I came out it seemed like I became a much happier person. There are many reasons why people are afraid of coming out. For some, it is the fear of not being accepted. For me, it was the feeling of being a big disappointment to my family. It felt like at every family event I would get the dreaded question: so do you have a boyfriend yet? This question made me extremely uncomfortable. Is having a girlfriend not okay? Why do I need to have a boyfriend?

Sexual Acceptance: A recurring struggle in my life has always been clothing style. I never understood why boys dress a certain way and girls dress another way. Why does it have to be so specific? Why can boys have short hair but not girls? In society there is a big difference between cutting your hair short and having a so-called boy haircut. I feel like I never fully expressed my identity until I cut my hair short. My junior year I told my mom I wanted to cut my hair short. But she had a very different idea of what short meant. She expected me to get a very femine style and since she took me to the saloon that is exactly what I got. I knew this haircut was not me. I felt that my mom was putting pressure on me to conform as to what was expected for a girl. A few months later I went to a barber and got my hair faded in a stylish masculine hairstyle. When I got home my parents flipped. They started yelling at me, saying people would not accept my new hairstyle and that it would be hard for me to get a job. They thought I was being reckless and that I was not thinking of the consequences. Their reaction destroyed me. But I gathered the courage to be myself and told my parents that they should think more about how I feel and less about the expectations of others. I know now that they were simply trying to protect me, but at the time I perceived their reaction as not accepting the real me.

Pinewood saved me through my whole coming out process. At the same time my parents were yelling at me about a haircut, I would walk the hallways at school and have teachers and students compliment my new style. I felt like I was truly myself for the first time, and I was accepted for who I was. I felt a great sense of belonging and happiness. I could finally be who I was meant to be and no one cared that I was different. In fact, I was somewhat of a pioneer for my tiny high school. After I officially came out more people, gay and bi-sexual kids, started showing who they really were. The whole school, students and faculty, accepted it with warmth and nothing but support. Pinewood was my sanctuary.

One of the biggest moments in my life was my senior year prom. I already had my short haircut and dressed in baggy clothes, but for prom I decided to wear a tuxedo instead of a dress. With only 50 kids per grade, we practically had the whole grade at one house for pre-prom pictures. When I walked in, I felt all the parents staring at me. I even heard a couple whispering behind my back, saying that they could not believe I showed up like that. I wore a black tuxedo with a gold vest and bowtie. I looked good and I felt good. All the students said I looked great and accepted me like it was normal. It was only the parents that made me uncomfortable. One of my friends had two lesbian parents, one of whom came up to me and said I looked great and that she was proud of me. She also explained that most of the parents were just in shock because what I did would have been unimaginable while they were in high school. I think they all knew that what I did took courage. It was in that moment that I knew I was 100% completely and utterly myself. ^My date and me on prom night :)
